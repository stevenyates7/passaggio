Free practice

Just you and the ladder. Sing a slow siren from your low range up through the rose break band and back down. Watch whether the dot stays bright and steady as it crosses E4 — a drop-out or colour flip is your voice flipping to falsetto instead of carrying a connected mix. The frequency readout shows octave glitches too: if it jumps but your pitch didn’t, the number lied, not you.

Stop the moment you feel tension in your throat, jaw, or neck. The top notes come from ease, not force — straining builds the wrong habit and risks damage.

Guided drills

Pick a drill and a starting root. The reference notes play on a soft tone and light up on the ladder as dashed targets — match them with your voice. Match a target and it locks bright. Nudge the root up a semitone at a time; stop climbing when A4 becomes your ceiling.

If a target sits above A4, skip it — don’t reach past your current ceiling to hit a reference note.

The 15-minute routine

Your daily flow, timed and in order — gentle warm-up first, break work in the middle, cool-down to finish. Each phase loads its drill automatically. Keep the mic on so your crossings still register.

Consistency beats intensity. Daily 15 minutes moves your break far more than an occasional long grind — and always stop if you feel strain.

Break power balancer

Your voice stays connected through E4 but goes quiet right in the break, then firms up above it. This camps on the three notes either side of that dip. Sustain each one; the bars show how much power you carried. The aim isn't loud — it's level: get E4 up to match D#4 and F4 so the dip fills in. Play the reference to guide your pitch.
